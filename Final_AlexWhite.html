def convert_ip(ip_raw):
    ip_raw = float(ip_raw)
    full_innings = int(ip_raw)
    partial = round(ip_raw - full_innings, 1)
    return full_innings + partial / 0.3

def clean_name(text):
    # NFD decompose so é becomes e + combining accent
    text = unicodedata.normalize('NFD', str(text))
    text = text.encode('ascii', errors='ignore').decode('ascii')

    text = re.sub(r"[^\w\s]", " ", text)

Problem 1: "Cheat Code" Lineup — Actual Points¶

Problem 1 Overview¶

If I had a magic crystal ball and knew exactly how every player would perform on September 19th, what lineup would I choose? This is the "cheat code" problem — I optimize using actual fantasy points scored as our objective function coefficients.

Decision Variables: x[i] = 1 if we select player i, 0 otherwise (binary)

Objective: Maximize total actual fantasy points scored by our 10-player lineup

Constraints:

  • Total salary ≤ $50,000
  • Exactly 10 players selected
  • Players from at least 2 different games
  • No more than 5 hitters from the same team
  • Exactly: 2P, 1C, 1B, 1 2B, 1 3B, 1 SS, 3 OF

Model Setup¶

In [9]:
model1 = pe.ConcreteModel()

# Decision variable: 1 if player i is selected, 0 else
model1.x = pe.Var(players, domain=pe.Binary)

Objective Function¶

In [10]:
model1.obj = pe.Objective(
    expr  = sum(dk.loc[i, 'actual_points'] * model1.x[i] for i in players),
    sense = pe.maximize
)

The objective maximizes the total fantasy points scored by the chosen lineup. Each player's actual points from September 19th serve as the coefficient on their binary selection variable — just like the profit coefficients in the planes and fertilizer problems from class.

# One selection per player — prevents multi-position players from filling more tha 1 slot
model1.OnePerPlayer = pe.ConstraintList()
for player in dk['player_name'].dropna().unique():
    idx = list(dk[dk['player_name'] == player].index)
    if len(idx) > 1:
        model1.OnePerPlayer.add(sum(model1.x[i] for i in idx) <= 1)

# 4. No more than 5 hitters from the same team
hitters = dk[dk['Roster Position'] != 'P']

In [14]:
# 5. Players must come from at least 2 different games
games = dk['game_id'].unique()
model1.y = pe.Var(games, domain=pe.Binary)

model1.GameLink = pe.ConstraintList()
for g in games:
    game_idx = list(dk[dk['game_id'] == g].index)
    model1.GameLink.add(
        sum(model1.x[i] for i in game_idx) <= len(game_idx) * model1.y[g]
    )

model1.MinGames = pe.Constraint(
    expr = sum(model1.y[g] for g in games) >= 2
)

I have 5 constraint groups:

  • SalaryCap- total salary under $50,000
  • RosterSize- exactly 10 players
  • Position constraints- exact DraftKings roster slots — multi-position players (like Shohei) show up in multiple position lists, so they can fill either slot
  • TeamCaps- uses a ConstraintList loop over all teams to cap hitters at 5 per team — the same looping pattern we used for resource constraints in class
  • GameLink- is a linking constraint just like the y variables in the planes problem — y[g]=1 forces the game to be "active", and I needed at least 2 active games

Problem 2: Predictive Lineup — Historical Strategy¶

Problem 2 Overview¶

For problem 2 I am going to build a smarter lineup without the crystal ball. Instead of using actual points, we need a strategy based on historical data.

My Strategy: I'm going to adjust each player's expected points using things:

  1. Home field advantage — players perform better at home. I will apply a bonus multiplier to players playing at home on September 19th.

  2. Opponent quality — players perform better against weaker pitching staffs. We will pull 2025 season ERA for each team's pitching staff from Baseball Reference and boost players facing high-ERA (weak) pitching.

The idea is: a player's true expected value on any given day is not just their season average — it depends on where they are playing and who they are playing against. This is better than just AvgPointsPerGame.

Build Adjusted Points¶

Here I combine home field advantage and opponent ERA into 1 single adjusted expected points value for each player. Here's why:

  • Took each player's opponent from the Game Info column
  • Looked up the opponent's ERA — higher ERA = weaker pitching, which is a better matchup for hitters
  • Applied a home bonus of 5% to players playing at home
  • Applied an ERA adjustment: for every 0.5 ERA above league average, added 3% to expected points (capped at +15%)

2b.) What is your predictive strategy and how does it compare to the naive approach of just selecting players based on average daily points?

Our strategy adjusted each player's expected value using two things: home field advantage (a 5% bonus for home hitters, 4% for home pitchers) and opponent pitching quality based on 2025 season ERA. Players facing weak pitching staffs above the league average ERA received up to a 15% boost, while those facing elite pitching were discounted.

By accounting for home field advantage and opponent ERA, we selected a considerably stronger lineup than someone simply picking the highest average scorers.

The cheat code scored 268.15 points — over 3x our predictive score — which shows how much variance exists in daily fantasy even with a smart strategy. Without perfect information there is always a ceiling to how well any predictive model can do on a single night. Over many contests though, consistently incorporating factors like home field and opponent ERA would be expected to compound that 71% edge into a significant advantage over naive players.
